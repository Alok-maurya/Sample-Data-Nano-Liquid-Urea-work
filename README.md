# Sample-Data-Nano-Liquid-Urea-work
The data is a part of the research work, where we have tested the effectivness of the nano liquid urea on potato crop. 

Titled: "Assessing Potato Nitrogen Status Under Nano-liquid Urea Using Spectral Response and Biophysical Traits"

Authors details:
Alok Kumar Maurya1, Amey Pathak1,*, Kamlesh Narayan Tiwari1
1Agricultural and Food Engineering Department, Indian Institute of Technology Kharagpur, West Bengal, India- 721302
*Corresponding Author Email: (amey@agfe.iitkgp.ac.in)


Abstract:
The potato (Solanum tuberosum) is highly susceptible to abiotic stress due to its shallow roots, fast vegetative growth, and bulky tuber formation. Therefore, precise nitrogen (N) assessment is essential for optimizing yield. In recent years, farmers have gained access to several new nitrogenous fertilizers, including Nano-Liquid Urea [NLU, 4% w/v], which is being promoted as an alternative to conventional prilled urea and to reduce environmental consequences. Although numerous studies across various crops have evaluated the effectiveness of NLU, most have focused primarily on biophysical crop traits, with limited attention given to leaf-level spectral responses. To address this gap, the present study assesses NLU's capacity to meet potato nitrogen requirements by integrating spectral responses and vegetation indices with supporting biophysical trait measurements, and by using statistical analysis (Two-Way ANOVA and Tukey’s HSD test). Accordingly, a two-year field experiment (2023-25) with three nitrogen treatments was conducted: T1-100% water requirement with recommended conventional urea, T2-100% water requirement without nitrogen, and T3-100% water requirement with NLU. Canopy spectral data were acquired with a handheld spectroradiometer, along with complementary biophysical measurements. Analysis demonstrated that spectral response-based assessment effectively captures potato nitrogen status. Specifically, the healthy crop (T1) exhibited lower reflectance in the green region and higher reflectance in the near-infrared (NIR) region, whereas N-deficient crops (T2 and T3) showed the inverse pattern. First-order derivative analysis, variance analysis, and PCA were employed to identify spectral bands most sensitive to nitrogen treatment differences. The key findings are as follows: (1) NLU proved ineffective in meeting potato nitrogen requirements, producing a response similar to the N-deficient treatment (T2) characterized by pale green leaves, whereas the conventional nitrogen treatment (T1) remained effective, exhibiting dark green foliage; (2) Eight spectral bands (556-560 nm, 570 nm, 638 nm, 675-678 nm, 702-703 nm, 706-711 nm, 760-763 nm, and 929-932 nm), were identified as sensitive wavelengths for assessing nitrogen status; (3) Vegetation indices derived from these sensitive wavelengths, including NDRE, NDRE1, VOG2, GNDVI, GNDVI1, CIgreen, MTCI, RRE, NDVI760,638, and NDVI932,638, effectively differentiated nitrogen-deficiency; (4) These indices distinguished treatment effects as early as 20 days after germination; (5) Furthermore, T3-treated plants exhibited biophysical traits comparable to T2, including plant height ≤35 cm, leaf count ≤11, and tuber yield ≤16.25 t ha⁻¹, representing over 44% yield reduction relative to the potential yield. Whereas T1-treated plants showed superior growth and yield, with plant height >50 cm, leaf count ≥16, and tuber yield >25 t ha⁻¹, (6) Treatment effects were highly significant (p < 0.01) for all traits. Year effects were significant for leaf count (p < 0.05) and yield (p < 0.01), with variable interaction effects. Tukey’s test showed T1 differed from T2 and T3, while T2 and T3 were at par. These findings indicate that spectral characterization provides a robust method for assessing crop N status, where NLU, applied at the tested dose and strategy, failed to meet crop nitrogen requirements. The study underscores the necessity of integrating spectral characterization with biophysical and statistical validation for comprehensive fertilizer efficacy evaluation in precision agriculture.

Keywords:  Nitrogen, Potato Crop, Spectral Response, Nano-liquid Urea, PCA, First Order Derivatives, Vegetation Indices
